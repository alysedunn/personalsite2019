I am a 'self taught' software engineer. That is to say: I don't have any formal education in computer science. I received a bachelor's in Business Administration from the University of Michigan in 2008, and then proceeded to teach myself how to code years after I graduated.

As a 'self-taught' engineer, I frequently get asked the question: 'How did you do it? How did you just teach yourself how to code, with no formal education, and then get a job doing it full-time?' I get this question so frequently that I posed it to five of my NYC-based, self-taught colleagues, in an attempt to provide a more comprehensive resource on how myself and others have successfully made this transition, and what advice they'd give to others who want to do the same. Their responses ranged from moving to downright hilarious. Read on for more information about how five different individuals made this increasingly coveted career move.


Q: What inspired you to learn how to code?

Shoaib: My initial failure inspired me to code. In short, back in 2014, I had a side business making websites for local small businesses. Since I wasn't a developer (yet), I shipped the development work overseas. On one project, the developer started BSing me —saying 'Oh this part of the site takes 3 months to make...' I was not a programmer, therefore, could not verify whether the job really took as long as he was claiming. Ultimately, the project failed and I had to return the client's money. The fact that I didn't know enough to manage a technical person angered me, and I decided to start learning how to code myself, so that I could have more control over the development process next time.

Richard: I actually learned to code when I was around ten years old. I taught myself BASIC, and then Pascal. I don't remember exactly what drew me into it except that we had a computer (this was 1980 —we had one of the first Apples) that my dad used for telecommuting, and I liked video games and wanted to learn how to create them. Also I liked solving puzzles. Then I got really into theater in high school, and stopped programming around the age of fifteen. Twenty-five years later, I was doing theater and working in New York as a production graphic designer at a newspaper (I did print layout —we used computers, but not the internet), and I slowly began to gravitate back to my early love of programming because a) it provided a way to solve problems, both at work (through using JavaScript to script the layout program Adobe InDesign) and outside work, where I created an attendance app for my recreational soccer team, and b) because I began to crave having a day job that would stretch my brain more than the one I was in. I still liked solving puzzles! As for whether the knowledge I had from when I was a kid helped me get back into it so many years later, the answer is, I have no idea. The programming languages and the ecosystems around them are very different today, but I imagine that some of the basic concepts I learned in the 80s remained helpful.

Q: How did you learn how to code?

Shoaib: I started learning online, first from w3schools, then from codecademy. Eventually, I also took part-time evening classes at New York Code & Design Academy, a comparatively new coding bootcamp.

Richard: In round one, starting when I was 10, I was almost entirely self-taught, from manuals. There were a few other inputs to my early programming education, but I only barely remember them. I took a few lessons with a game programmer who gave instruction to young programmers (but whose name I forget). I also visited computer centers, where I imagine they had people I could ask questions of, if not classes of some kind. In round two, when I was 40, I learned by reading books and things on the internet, doing a couple of Coursera courses, doing small code projects at work (and on the Abobe forums from the ten people in the world who know what they're doing in terms of scripting InDesign), going to Meetups that I barely understood, and creating side projects and putting them up on Github. In addition, I also took three short classes at NYU continuing education (two on JavaScript and one on CSS and designing for mobile). The mobile course was pretty good. The first JavaScript course was very bad, but the second one was really good and instilled in me the belief that I could actually do this for a living. But again, almost all of my learning was done outside of those classes. The main thing I got out of them was inspiration and affirmation, from the second JavaScript class. After I had been a professional software developer for a y ear, I did a three month programmer's retreat called Hacker School (now called the Recurse Center), which was really fun and which I think helped me to jump to another level when I was looking for my next job.

Q: When did you start learning how to code?

Shoaib: I think I started the coding bootcamp in May 2015

Richard: Round one: Around 1980. Round two: It was a really slow process with a lot of stops and starts. I began around 2007, doing hobby projects and small scripting tasks at work. Some time after that, I remember a two-year period there where I gave up on the idea of actually doing it for a living, because I didn't want it to colonize my mind. Then I changed my mind and started thinking seriously about doing it professionally. At the beginning of 2012, I committed myself to that path by going down to half-time at my job, in an irreversible way (I got a friend of mine the gig of doing half my job, and told her —and only her —what my ultimate plan was, and that she could have my whole job eventually, if she could stick with the part-time gig for a while). I felt I needed the extra time every week to ramp up my skills, but once I took that step, I was on track to either run out of savings or land a software development gig.

Q: How long did it take you to secure your first coding job, from the time you started looking, to the time that you received an offer that you accepted?

Shoaib: So I came from a very non technical background. After I completed the bootcamp in the summer of 2015, getting a coding job was not even my priority. I was not confident enough to apply for a technical job. I was happy enough being able to make static websites and understand code a little better. However, right after completing the coding bootcamp, I started teaching others how to code in a small paid setting. That gave me the experience needed to get hired by CUNY in 2016, teaching TLC laws. Meanwhile, I started my Master's in Computer Science in 2016, and graduated in May 2018. After I graduated, I started actively looking for a development job in September 2018, then finally accepted an offer in December 2018.

Richard: Six months, although I only applied for a few random things for the first three months, during which time I didn't feel that I was really ready. Once I did feel I was ready, I got more systematic about the job search, and it took me another three months from that point.

Q: What's the most important thing you wish you knew/understood when you first started learning how to code that you know now?

Shoaib:If I knew then what I know now, I would have stuck to one tech stack. For example, it's better to select a stack such as javascript, then become an expert on it, before moving onto a different stack.

Richard:

  1. Git isn't as scary as it seems at first. Whenever you find yourself frustrated trying to figure out what it's doing, go to this site. It won't help you with your immediate issue, but it will make you feel better.
  2. It is very common for people to be unable to imagine themselves as "real programmers". The lack of ability to make that cognitive leap definitely affected me, for years, before I got the confidence to get serious. And I am literally a while male who started coding in the 80s, so you know if someone with all my pre-existing privileges and advantages had a problem with that, it must be a common issue. Now that I have been getting paid to program for the past five years, I cannot stress highly enough that there is NO SUCH THING as a "real programmer". There are only gradations on a continuum of experience levels. Also, read this. It's a great article about what "real programmers" look like, with a plot twist in the middle.
  3. Don't be intimidated by the jerks that you meet in this business (and there will be a few). You can safely ignore them and their advice. The very best programmers are often nice, humble people. If they didn't know how to listen, they would never have gotten to where they are.
