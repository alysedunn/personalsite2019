I am a 'self taught' software engineer. That is to say: I don't have any formal education in computer science. I received a bachelor's in Business Administration from the University of Michigan in 2008, and then proceeded to teach myself how to code years after I graduated.

As a 'self-taught' engineer, I frequently get asked the question: 'How did you do it? How did you just teach yourself how to code, with no formal education, and then get a job doing it full-time?'

I get this question so frequently that I posed it to five of my NYC-based self-taught colleagues, in an attempt to provide a more comprehensive resource on how myself and others have successfully made this transition, and what advice they'd give to others who want to do the same. Their responses ranged from moving to downright hilarious. Read on for more information about how five different individuals made this increasingly coveted career move.


Q: What inspired you to learn how to code?

Shoaib: My initial failure inspired me to code. In short, back in 2014, I had a side business of building websites for small businesses. Since I was not a developer, I was shipping the development work overseas to some developers I knew. One of my projects failed miserably because the developer was BSing me that ''ohh this part of the site takes 3 months to make...". I was not a programmer, therefore, could not verify whether the job really took as long as he was claiming. Ultimately the project failed and I had to return all the money to the client. The fact that I didn't't know enough to manage a technical person angered me, and I decided to start learning how to code myself, so that I could have more control over the development process next time.

Q: How long did it take you to secure your first coding job, from the time you started looking, to the time that you received an offer that you accepted?

Shoaib: So I came from a very non technical background. After I completed the bootcamp in the summer of 2015, getting a coding job was not even my priority. I was not confident enough to apply for a technical job. I was happy enough being able to make static websites and understand code a little better. However, right after completing the coding bootcamp, I started teaching others how to code in a small paid setting. That gave me the experience needed to get hired by CUNY in 2016, teaching TLC laws. Meanwhile, I started my Master's in Computer Science in 2016, and graduated in May 2018. After I graduated, I started actively looking for a development job in September 2018, then finally accepted an offer in December 2018.
